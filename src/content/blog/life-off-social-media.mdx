## The Feed Was Always On

Before the experiment, I didn't think of myself as addicted to social media. I had accounts, I checked them, but I wasn't *that person* — the one who scrolls through dinner.

Except I was. I just didn't notice because the habit had become invisible, woven into every gap between tasks: the two minutes waiting for coffee to brew, the idle moment before a meeting began, the first ten seconds after waking up.

When I finally deleted the apps, I expected boredom. What I didn't expect was the sensation of **space** — something between relief and mild panic.

---

## What You Lose (And Learn to Live Without)

There's no idealising the transition. Leaving social media means genuinely losing things.

### The ambient awareness of others

Social media provides a low-resolution but continuous signal of what your circle is doing: who moved cities, who had a baby, who is angry about something. When that signal disappears, you notice how dependent you'd become on it. You actually have to *ask people* how they are.

This sounds obvious. It isn't easy. Many friendships, it turns out, had calcified into mutual observation rather than conversation.

### The validation loop

Posting something and feeling the small dopamine hit of likes is real. It's not a moral weakness — it's a mechanism deliberately engineered by teams of hundreds. You will miss it. The creative work you do afterwards, shared with no one or very few people, will feel different: quieter, more uncertain, occasionally more honest.

### The news cycle

Leaving social media doesn't mean leaving the news (RSS readers and direct newsletters still exist), but it does mean losing the *social layer* on top of the news — the hot takes, the performance of being informed, the group identity that comes with taking the right position loudly enough.

That layer, it turns out, was generating more anxiety than information.

---

## What You Gain

### Time, first

The estimates around average social media use (two to three hours daily) felt abstract until I had that time back. I started reading again — not articles, but books. The first month I finished four. The cumulative attention of a sustained read is qualitatively different from any amount of scrolling, and the difference becomes obvious fast.

### A cleaner relationship with your own opinions

Without a constant audience, you stop drafting positions for public consumption. You think something, sit with it, and sometimes change your mind without anyone knowing. The opinions you do form tend to be softer, more provisional, less performed.

### Presence in physical spaces

The instinct to reach for a phone in any moment of social friction doesn't disappear overnight, but it fades. Meals become meals. Conversations end when the other person leaves rather than when you pick up your device.

---

## The Hard Parts Nobody Talks About

### You become less legible to your social world

People assume you know things you don't. You miss event invitations posted only to private groups. You're excluded from in-jokes. Some of this is trivial; some of it genuinely stings.

The solution is imperfect: tell people directly that you're not on the platforms, and accept that some social infrastructure simply won't include you. For most people, this is a reasonable trade. For some — particularly anyone whose professional life is tightly coupled to visibility — the calculus is harder.

### The mirror problem

Social media, for all its distortions, does reflect *something* back at you. Without it, some people feel a loss of self-definition, particularly if their identity had become entangled with a persona maintained for an audience. Re-anchoring identity to things that don't require an audience (relationships, craft, daily habit, physical place) is slower and more uneven than anyone warns you about.

---

## A Note on "Intentionality"

The point was never to become some pure, screen-free ascetic. The goal was to stop being on social media *by default* — to stop treating the feed as the natural state of waiting for something else.

After a year without it, the best description I can offer is this: my attention feels more like mine. Not fully — the attention economy operates through dozens of mechanisms besides social media — but substantially more so.

If that sounds appealing, there's likely only one way to find out if it's true for you too.
